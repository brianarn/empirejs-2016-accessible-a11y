Accessibility

Why do we care?

The web is pervasive

Think about how much you use the internet on a daily basis.

Now imagine that for some reason, you're unable to use a mouse.

How usable do you think things are?

Improved UX

Better accessibility helps everyone

Many people think of a11y as primarily for blind people, but it can improve things for many people.

  • Those with color blindness
  • People with limited gross motor skills
  • Vi users who threw away their mouse.

It's THE LAW

 

  • Americans with Disabilities Act
  • Section 508
  • 1990: ADA was enacted, prohibiting discrimination by places of public accomodation
  • 2005: National Federation of the Blind and Target tried to work it out
  • 2006: NFB sued Target for their inaccessible website, citing it as a place of public accomodation
  • 2008: Settlement reached, $6mil settlement fund was established

Section 508 matters if you ever care to sell services to the US Gov't

Principles & Guidelines

Principles: POUR

Guidelines: WCAG 2.0

POUR

  • Perceivable
  • Operable
  • Understandable
  • Robust

Perceivable

Can everyone consume your content?

Examples:

  • Text alternatives for non-text content
  • Captions, other alternatives for multimedia content
  • Visible to users of varying levels of sight
  • Functions well with varying ATs

Operable

How well does your app work without a mouse?

Examples:

  • Keyboard navigation (tab, spacebar, enter, etc)
  • Touch interfaces

Understandable

Are they getting what you're putting down?

Okay, maybe a bit snarky, but I hope it helps make the point in a way.

  • Is the content readable? Free of typos?
  • Is the content succinct? Long-form content can be fatiguing to users of ATs.

Robust

Write once, accessible everywhere

The idea here is that it works well in a broad variety of environments. Different browsers, ATs, etc.

Guidelines: WCAG 2.0

POUR codified into rule sets

Three tiers: A, AA, AAA

Example levels

  • 3.1.1 (A): Language of page can be programmatically determined
  • 3.1.2 (AA): Language of passages / phrases can be programmatically determined, with some exceptions
  • 3.1.3 (AAA): Mechanism available to identify words or phrases used in an unusual way (jargon etc)

This slide is quite verbose, but the concept is there. These rules clearly iterate on one another.

Not all WCAG guidelines work this way, but this particular example is really nice for demonstrating the iteration.

Another example: Captions on video.

  • A: Having text captions for pre-recorded audio content.
  • AA: Having text captions provided for live audio content.
  • AAA: Sign language interpretation for pre-recorded audio content.

Where should we be?

  • Level A: Bare minimum
  • Level AA: Great!
  • Level AAA: Good luck with that

Most groups are happy to pursue AA. It's pretty simple to get to A without much effort. Mostly.

Semantic Markup

LINKS ARE NOT BUTTONS

Different semantics

Browsers and ATs will represent links and buttons very differently.

Links are intended for navigation, buttons are intended for interactions. Use the appropriate element.

LINKS ARE NOT BUTTONS

Buttons can be styled, don't be lazy

LINKS ARE NOT BUTTONS

Make sure to set type="button" for pure JS interaction

SPANS ARE NOT BUTTONS

NEITHER ARE DIVS

Use the right element for the job!

Put alt attributes on images

alt="" is ok!

ATs will recognize an empty alt to mean that content is meant for presentation, and will skip it

Colors

Oft ignored, but important!

Color Usage

  • Color should not be the only meaningful visual
  • Contrast matters!

This is basically WCAG 2.0 section 1.4

Contrast of 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text (18pt and up)

Logos are considered exempt from contrast rules
